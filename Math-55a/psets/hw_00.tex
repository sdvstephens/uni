\qs{}{Let \( f : X \to  Y  \) be a map of sets. A function \( g : Y \to  X \) is called a left (resp. right) inverse of \( f \) if \( g \circ f = \text{id}_X \) (resp. \( f \circ g = \text{id}_Y\)). 
  \begin{enumerate}[(a)]
  \item Show that \( f \) admits a right inverse if an only if \( f \) is surjective. Show that, in general, a right inverse is not unique.
  \item Show that \( f \) admits a left inverse if an only if \( f \) is injeective. Show that, in general, a left inverse is not unique. 
\end{enumerate}} 
\pf{Proof}{\pf{(a) \(\implies \)}{Let \( f \circ g = \text{id}_Y \), then for all \( y, \, g(y)=x\) we have \( f(g(y))=f(x) \implies y=f(x), \, \forall y\)}
\pf{(a) \( \impliedby \)}{For all \( y \), there exists an \( x \) such that \( f(x)=y \). Then, \( f(x)=f(g(y)) \implies y=f(g(y)), \, \forall y \implies f\circ g = \text{id}_Y \)}
\pf{(a). In general a right inverse is not unqiue.}{Consider \( f:X \surjto Y  \) such that \( X=\mathbb{N} \) and \( Y=\{0\}. \) Then there exist infinitely many right inverses since any \( x \in  X \) can map to the only \( y \in  Y \).}
\pf{(b)\( \implies \)}{Given \( g \circ f = \text{id}_X \). Let \( f(x_{1})=f(x_{2}) \) for arbitrary \( x_{1},x_{2}\in X \). This implies that \( g(f(x_{1}))=g(f(x_{2})) \), but since \( g \circ f =\text{id}_X\) we get \( x_{1}=x_{2} \).}
\pf{(b)\( \impliedby \)}{Given injectivity. Let \( y_{1}=f(x_{1}) \) for arbitrary \( x_{1},y_{1} \). Then \( g(y_{1})=g(f(x_{1})) \) and since \( g(y_1)=x_{1} \), we have \( x_{1}=g(f(x_{1})), \forall x \in X \implies g \circ f = \text{id}_X\). THIS IS WRONG!!! CORRECT THIS!!!} 
\pf{(b). In general a left inverse is not unique.}{Consider \( f :X \surjto Y \) where \( X = \{0\}, \, Y=\mathbb{N}   \). Any left inverse must have \( g(0)=0 \), but \( g(n) \) for \( n\neq 0 \) can be anything, giving infinitely many left inverses.}
